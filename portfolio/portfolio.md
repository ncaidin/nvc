## Getting Started

I am starting with Love.

A key distinction (KD) is between love as an action vs love as a need
and a feeling.

Feelings change moment-by-moment and last, at most, 90 seconds, unless
we sustain them through our thoughts or stories. When our need for Love
is met, whether through giving or receiving, we may feel peace, comfort,
joy, excitement, arousal, affection, aliveness, amazement, gratitude, or
something else entirely.

Needs are expressions of our deepest values, our life energy. Is there
any word that describes life energy better than Love? I don't know how
many needs exist. A hundred? A thousand? But if I had to choose just
one, I would choose Love. Perhaps all other needs are simply threads in
a tapestry of interconnected energy that leads back to this one.

We can take actions motivated by Love, intending to meet another's need
for Love. But those actions may not land as intended. Actions are
strategies; attempts to meet needs.

Strategies are the bridge between the Invisible (our needs, life energy,
and intention) and the Visible (our actions, our being). This movement,
from the unseen to the expressed, is the beginning of the dance to get
everyone's needs met.

Play is a love language for me, especially in connection with others. It
allows us to meet in a space beyond judgment, beyond story where
presence itself is enough. In this way, Play is communion.

Jim Morrison\'s words, for me, capture the spirit of Love, and the
difficulty of defining it. So while I can explain a key distinction
about Love, I don\'t believe my explanation fully captures its nature.

## Key Differentiations (KDs)

I addressed the KD of love as an action, need, or feeling earlier in the
document. In this section I will explore four more that hold meaning and
challenge for me.

### Idiomatic (Street Giraffe) vs classical (formal) Giraffe. 

Classical Giraffe is based on OFNR, essentially sensing feelings and
needs and expressing them explicitly. Idiomatic, or Street Giraffe, is
saying things in a way that is less formal and might especially be more
appropriate for connecting to those to whom OFNR feels foreign or rigid.

I chose this KD because I've experienced firsthand how painful it can be
when a well-intentioned attempt to translate Classical Giraffe into
Idiomatic lands as advice or strategy instead of connection. I've also
noticed that guessing feelings and needs doesn't always land, and other
approaches I've tried to connect seem to vary in effectiveness.

I used to think about Idiomatic Giraffe like using Google Translate for
NVC. You just take the feelings and needs guesses; plug it in; and
voilÃ¡, the perfect Street Giraffe translation appears. But the work of
real connection isn't about plugging words into a formula, it's about
sensing what is alive and responding from that energy.

How do I know the differentiation? If I am stuck in my languaging, using
feelings and needs, but it is clearly not landing, then I may be leaning
too heavily on OFNR. If I am staying focused on what's in my heart, and
sensing what is in the other person's heart, and speaking in a more
fluid way, then I am engaging in Idiomatic Giraffe.

A good example of Idiomatic Giraffe comes from a Rita-Ism:

> *\| A woman tells Rita that her husband was diagnosed with cancer. Her
> empathic response : "Difficult road ahead."*

What makes this Idiomatic Giraffe?

- Not explicitly guessing feelings and needs.

- Sensing what is most important to the woman, in that moment.

- Using simple, heartfelt, honest words to convey connection and
  presence.

In both OFNR and Idiomatic Giraffe you want to sense where the other
person is, and sense what is in your own heart, the difference is that
the delivery of Idiomatic Giraffe is less likely to be received as
formulaic, or foreign. OFNR might be received that way, even if it is
done skillfully, because it is not the norm in our culture. That being
said, sometimes OFNR lands beautifully, even in its structured form. It
depends on the moment. When to use OFNR is also an intuitive choice.

How to share? : I can think of a couple of relationships in which I have
trouble connecting. I intend to spend some time "workshopping" how I can
stay connected but use different words and then try and apply them to
daily life. If I can live it, I can model it. If I can model it, I can
share/teach it.

### Interdependence vs dependence or independence. 

I chose this KD because I see my path forward as centered on
interdependence to effect social change.

- Independence means relying solely on oneself and functioning without
  depending on others.

- Dependence signifies a reliance on someone or something else to meet
  needs.

- Interdependence refers to a mutual reliance where individuals depend
  on each other to achieve goals.

Independence and dependence are illusions to some extent.
Interdependence more accurately reflects reality. Even in tasks that
seem independent, like driving to work, eating a meal, or writing on a
computer, I rely on the labor, resources, and creativity of others.
Likewise, these systems rely on my participation to function.

A new insight for me is that dependence, independence, and
interdependence are not fixed states but more like a continuum that we
live on, constantly changing and in flux moment-to-moment. The factors
that come up for me most when considering this continuum are external
circumstances, choice, impact, and power. External circumstances include
health events, aging, cultural norms, and institutional structures.

For example, in August 2024, I fainted at home, injured myself, and had
to call 911. I was dependent on emergency responders and hospital staff
for care. Yet, even in that dependence, I had choices. Whether to call
for help, follow medical advice, or stay home.

Interdependence invites us to acknowledge and accept our circumstances
while staying connected to choice.

Out of the spirit of independence, I make a thousand choices daily
without asking anyone's advice or permission.

Interdependence invites us to be conscious that our choices may impact
people, animals, plants, and the environment, locally and globally,
whether intentionally or not.

*How I live interdependence* -

I practice interdependence in my cohousing community by giving,
receiving, and co-creating a heart-centered environment. Though
preparing to leave, I carry that spirit of collaboration with me.

Another way to practice interdependence is to be more conscious of my
choices. As I write this, I notice it doesn't feel like play, and I have
some inner resistance. A place to come back to explore.

*How I share interdependence -*

I practice interdependence in NVC workshops and practice groups by
explicitly inviting shared ownership of our learning and mutual support.
I encourage participants to make requests when something in the session
isn't working for them, and I adjust my facilitation based on community
feedback and needs.

### Observation vs observation mixed with evaluation

I chose this KD because observation plays a crucial role in my
self-empathy practice. One way I use it is when reading texts or emails.
If I feel triggered, I've found that waiting before rereading helps me
see the message more as a pure observation rather than through the lens
of my initial reaction and interpretations. Interpretations can lead to
emotional reactions that can stir anger, discouragement, or
disconnection. When I shift to pure observation, I gain a clearer sense
of the author's intention, which in turn cultivates curiosity and deeper
understanding.

*How I know it:*

Observation is what a camera can observe of external behaviors and
events. For example, you can't observe anger, because that is an
internal state. But you can observe a furrowed brow, a red face, a
widened posture and that their voice got louder. Interpretation is a way
to try and make sense of these things by assigning labels like angry,
flustered, etc.

*How I live it:*

In my self empathy practice, I notice my feelings, body sensations, and
thoughts as they arise. I start with a clear observation, free from
interpretation, whether it's an external event, like the text of an
email or something someone said, or an internal reaction, like
self-criticism (jackal in) or judgment of another (jackal out).

If I begin with an external observation, I follow the feelings it
stimulates and track the energy in my body until I connect with my
needs. If I start with a critical voice, I empathize with it rather than
resist it. The Educator/Chooser model helps me recognize the needs
behind my inner judgments and the needs behind my choices. When I
connect with all the needs present, I often experience a softening, a
shift toward greater self connection and ease.

*How I share it:*

In NVC intros, Eileen McAvoy and I often play an observation game where
one of us silently acts out various activities. We then ask participants
what they observed and categorize their responses using the OEFP
framework: Observation, Evaluation, Feeling, and Purpose. This exercise
often leads to aha moments as participants notice how naturally
interpretations arise and how they can sometimes create disconnection.
It's a playful yet eye-opening way to explore the distinction between
observation and evaluation.

### Respect for authority vs. fear of authority

Relationship with authority is a key learning edge for me, potentially
deepening my growth and fostering hope around this issue. It also ties
to the FirstGen NVCer retreat, where viewing the FirstGeners as
authorities on NVC could be easy. This event may challenge how I choose
to express myself, either holding back or sharing in ways that might
unintentionally feel disconnected from others.

The word "authority" has multiple dimensions:

- It can derive from expertise and knowledge, where authority is
  recognized as a result of skills and contributions.

- It can also be granted by a structural hierarchy or a role with
  responsibility in an organization.

These two forms of authority seem distinct to me, yet both present
opportunities and challenges in my relationship to power.

*How do I know the difference?*

Respect for authority acknowledges expertise or the responsibility of a
given role, but it requires staying connected to the humanity of the
person in authority. If I feel fear or an urge to submit, rebel, or
hide, these are signs that I may not be connecting with their humanity
or my own.

For example, in my previous role as a program manager at the Linux
Foundation, I often experienced challenging interactions with Steve G.,
a volunteer community treasurer for one of my open source projects. I
experienced his behavior as bullying but felt too intimidated to speak
up, often resorting to the conflict strategy of accommodating. This
situation left me disconnected from my own needs, leading to ineffective
team dynamics and personal pain. When I attempted to seek support from
my manager, I found him to be conflict-avoidant, reinforcing the
pattern.

This painful experience highlights the Key Differentiation (KD): when
respect for authority comes from connection, I feel grounded in my
perspective and sense of choice. However, fear of authority often arises
when I feel powerless, disconnected from my needs, and caught in
patterns like submission or rebellion.

Connecting with the humanity of those in authority opens up greater
possibilities for mutual respect and collaboration. I can value their
expertise, role, and responsibility while maintaining my own sense of
agency and interdependence.

*How do I live this difference?*

As a leader, I aim to earn authority through my behaviors, knowledge,
skills, and actions that add value to my community. In this sense,
authority is a gift, not something gained through coercion. I see it as
my responsibility to reduce the intimidation that authority can create,
bridging the distance it may cause by fostering shared humanity and
values, especially with marginalized groups who may have an additional
level of trauma with which to contend.

At the FirstGen Retreat, I intend to live this value by respecting the
FirstGeners' authority, grounded in their experience and knowledge. At
the same time, I aim to challenge myself to express myself
authentically, even though this may push against my fear of "using too
many words" or my trauma around "being seen." Staying self-connected and
present will be my priority, trusting the words that come to me in the
moment. And, failing that, coming back to empathizing with myself,
reconnecting, and accepting myself for who I am if things don't go the
way I would prefer.

How do I share this difference?

This very much feels connected to how I share independence and model
leadership and authority.
